Canadians & Coffee

The double-double (two creams, two sugars) became a Canadian icon in the 1990s through Tim Hortons. The beloved coffee and donut chain has transformed this order into a household phrase across Canada. This reflects a national taste for sweeter, creamier coffee. Surveys consistently show that fewer than one in five Canadians drink coffee black, while the majority add dairy and sugar.

No Cream, No Sugar

While most Canadians have perfected the art of coffee customization, there's a special kind of courage in ordering it straight up. Across the country, preferences shift from region to region. Some places are cream & sugar strongholds, while others celebrate the bold, unapologetic black coffee lifestyle. Preference for black coffee rises from east to west. From just 13% in Atlantic Canada to 22% in Saskatchewan and Manitoba.

Millions of Kilograms Imported Every Month

In June 2024 alone - Canada's coffee imports reached 20.5 million kilograms, led by Colombia (38.2%), Brazil (21.4%), Guatemala (12.9%), and Honduras (9.9%). Canada imported 5.1 million kilograms of roasted coffee from multiple countries and US states in June 2024, while decaffeinated coffee and alternatives represent smaller import volumes.

Hot Prices: Why Your Coffee Costs More This Year

August 2025 saw Canadian coffee prices spike 27.9% annually, driven by unfavorable weather in key producing regions (Colombia, Brazil, Honduras, Guatemala, Mexico, Peru). Despite recent hikes, coffee has historically beaten overall food inflation. Demand remains strong, with Canadian manufacturers shipping $292.8 million in products (July 2025).
